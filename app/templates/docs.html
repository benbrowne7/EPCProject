Tool Overview

The Heat Pump Ready App is a tool designed to provide users with insights into the state of heat pump adoption within the UK, a critical technology in the UK's drive to net zero across the economy, particularly in heating of buildings, where 'virtually all heat will need to be decarbonised'. [Heat and Buildings Strategy (BEIS)]

The Tool is targeted at individual homeowners interested in upgrading to a heat pump and policy makers (local council and authority district level) wanting information regarding energy performance and heat pump suitability at various geographic levels, to support heat pump programmes and related investment.

The various features of the tool and the datasets they rely on are detailed in different sections of the documents page.

The Tool was developed by Ben Browne, with the support of the University of Bristol, Dr Ruzanna Chitchyan and the Center for Sustainable Energy (CSE). A full thesis written during the initial development of the tool, detailing motivations, context and more technical aspects of the tool is available for download here: Heat Pump Tool Thesis.

Grid

The Grid Page attempts to provide insights into the national grids infrastructure at a local authority district level, specifically focusing on Primary substations which handle the distribution and voltage step down of electricity. The electricity distribution network is owned and operated by the National Grid, with 14 licensed distribution operators (DNOs) in Britain, owned by 6 different groups.

Primary Substation Capacity is available for some of these DNOs: National Grid (formerly Western Power), Northern Power, UK Power Networks and Electricity North West. The data incorporated into the tool includes Primary Substation names, Demand Headrooms (MVA), Firm Capacities (MVA), Current Maximum Demand (MVA), Lat/Long Coordinates and a Demand Headroom Rating (RED, AMBER, GREEN). The exception to this is Electricity North West, where Firm Capacities are not clear and the Demand Headroom Rating is manually set to 'RED' if headroom = 0, 'AMBER' if headroom less than 2 MVA and 'GREEN' otherwise. The link to these datasets are specified in the dataset section.



This is the main landing page for the 'Grid' part of the application. The large map shows Primary Substations from the 4 DNOs described above, displaying Demand Headroom and Firm Capacities information on hover. The map is plotted using the Plotly Express Library, using map data from mapbox. The highlighted red box shows the summed Firm Capacities and Demand Headroom figures, calculating total utilization as: (Total Firm Capacity - Total Demand Headroom) / Total Firm Capacity. Substation data from Electricity North West and the other 2 DNO groups (Scottish and Southern Electricity Networks, SP Energy Networks) are not included in these calculations.



A Local Authority District can be chosen, where the district boundaries are plotted onto the mapbox. The boundary data used is from the Office for National Statistics, for more information see the dataset section. A combined dictionary of substations {key:substation_name, value:[substation_data]} is then iterated through, with those not falling within the selected LAD boundaries being dropped. Those remaining are then plotted on the map, colour coded by the corresponding 'Demand Headroom RAG' Rating. Unfortunately, this means that primary substations that lie outside the geographical LAD boundary but supply areas of the district are not included.

The the highlighted box contains information regarding the primary substations within the district and can estimation as to the number of additional heat pumps the district could support. Assumptions made include: maximum demand of a domestic heat pump to be 1.7kW, a primary substation capacity utilization of 90%, no infrastructure improvements and no additional demand loads other than heat pumps. This number of additional heat pumps is then divided by the sum of the number of EPCs for the district, to give a approximate % of homes supported for the district. The (theoretical) required electricity to meet the demand for 10% of homes in the district with a heat pump is also shown.

Individual

The Individual page is targeted towards the individual home owner, looking to learn more about there homes energy performance and heat pump suitability.

The left hand side box contains a chloropleth map of England and Wales, seperated into local authority districts and coloured by average EPC rating or HPR (heat pump readiness) rating, switchable in the top left of the plot. Average EPC rating is calculated as an average of all certificates classified in that local authority district. These certificates were downloaded offline (See Datasets - EPC for more info). A HPR rating is also calculated for each home/certificate using various properties of the EPC: 'walls-energy-efficiency', 'floor-energy-efficiency', 'window-energy-efficiency', 'roof-energy-efficiency' and the overall 'current-energy-efficiency', the walls and roof components are weighted double. A HPR rating of over 1 corresponds to a 'likely suitable heat pump candidate', with a rating below 1 indicating the home may not be very suitable in its current form. The HPR rating is therefore an indication of a homes energy efficiency levels, specifically its insulation, an often important factor in heat pump suitability. A more comprehensive overview of the HPR calculation can be found in the written thesis, section 3.2.2.

Through the buttons on the top right of the map, the 'UK Heat Pump Adoption' button switches the maps dataset to 'heat pump installations under the RHI scheme' (see Datasets - Heat Pump Adoption)

The 2 plots for heat pump adoption are: 'Average Yearly % Increase in Installations since 2020' and 'Installed Heat Pumps Per 1000 People (RHI Scheme - 2022)', population counts for each district were taken from the Office for National Statistics. Both these plots colour code each local authority district by 'average % increase' and 'installations per 1000 people' respectively and are produced using the Bokeh Library.



Once a user has inputted a postcode and selected a property, an API request for the properties EPC is made (see Datasets - EPC). The highlighted red box contains basic EPC information for the property and associated recommended improvements. The 'Eligibility for Boiler Upgrade Scheme' section states 3 requirements: Valid Certificate (<10 years old) and no Outstanding Walls and Roof Insulation upgrade recommendations. Due to inconsistencies in the Recommendations API, eligibility for the last 2 requirements are determined by the walls and roof efficiency ratings on the EPC. These range from 'Very Poor' to 'Very Good', a rating of 'Average' is enough to meet the requirement set. A user would need to double check aganist there full EPC recomendations to ensure there are no outstanding wall/loft upgrades, a link to find this EPC is included. The properties calculated HPR rating is displayed in blue

The highlighted blue box includes some context/background for the displayed information on the right and a 'Confidence in EPC Metric': Low Confidence for Expired Certificates (10+ years old), Medium Confidence for Certificates 5-10 years old and High Confidence for Certificates less than 5 years old. Links to additional resources including information on heat pump grants/funding and finding MCS Certified Installers are also included. Nestas Heat Pump Cost Calculator is also embedded into the page, accessible through buttons in the top right of the box.



A user can then 'compare' their homes energy performance to those of their postcode and local authority district, by using the buttons in the top right of the box. This shows average EPC and HPR ratings for the postcode, calculated from data obtained through an API request for all EPCs for the postcode. Percentiles are also calculated for postcodes EPC and HPR ratings, showing where the individuals home lies in comparison. This is colour coded: 'red' for 0th -> 30th percentiles, 'amber' for 31st -> 70th percentiles and 'green' for the 71st -> 100th percentiles. The same is done for the properties local authority district, with the averages and percentiles having been precomputed using the full EPC dataset.

Local Authority District (LAD)

The LAD Page is targeted towards policy makers/officals and those interested in learning more about the UKs energy performance levels at a postcode/district level.

A user selects a Local Authority District from the drop down menu or inputs an ONS code. The highlighted red box contains information regarding average EPC/HPR for all LADs and compares the chosen LAD to all others in percentile terms for those 2 metrics. All averages and percentiles are precomputed using the entire EPC dataset for each LAD. The proportion of EPCs regarded as good candidates (HPR ratings over 1) and proportion of expired certificates for the LAD is also displayed.

The highlighted green box contains various energy performance maps for the selected LAD. Users can switch between a Postcode District map and Postcode Sector map, coloured by average EPC rating or average HPR rating. See Datasets - Geo Data for more information.

The graph pane is the lower half of the red box contains graphs with various insights into energy performance (see Datasets - EPC) for the district.

  • 'EPC Trend' Graph shows the average EPC rating for all certificates issued in that year.

  • 'EPC Y/Y' Graph shows the % change Year-over-Year between average EPC rating for all certificates issued in that year.

  • 'Age Distribution' Graph shows the distribution of the age of all EPCs, from 2008-2022.

Buttons in the green box switches content between 'Energy Performance' and 'Heat Pump Adoption' for the chosen LAD. The Heat Pump Adoption tab displays the average yearly % increase in Heat Pumps (under RHI) and average number of installations per 1000 people over all LADs. These figures for the chosen LAD are then shown below, as well as, where it lies in percentile terms when compared to all other local authority districts. The graph pane below displays heat pump installation data (see Datasets - Heat Pump Adoption) for the district. The left hand side box shows the England/Wales heat adoption map as described in Docs - Individual.

  • 'Heat Pump Installations' Graph shows the cumulative number of heat pump installations (under RHI scheme) by year, seperated by air source and ground source installations.

  • 'Annual % Increase in Installations' Graph shows the % increase each year in the number of heat pump installations under the RHI scheme. The white line at 47% represents the average annual % increase in installations required to reach the goverments target of 600,000 installations per year by 2028, from 2022 levels.

Datasets

Various datasets are used by the tool, often requiring small modifications and cleaning before being read in as a pandas dataframe. Some data is requested through APIs and is therefore the latest available, but many datasets are manual downloads and so may not always be the latest version.

Grid Data

  • National Grid Substation Capacity Map Data - Primary Substation Demand and Demand Headrooms

  • Northern Power Grid Demand Availability Map Data - Primary Substation Demand and Demand Headrooms. Data in .xlsx format, split into 'Northeast Demand' and 'Yorkshire Demand' tabs. These sheets are manually merged into a single .csv file, with demand headroom for each primary substation calculated as 'Firm Capacity (MVA)' - 'Maximum Demand (MVA)'.

  • UK Power Networks Primary Substation Distribution Areas Data - Primary Substation Demand and Demand Headrooms. This data specifies 2 firm capacities for Summer and Winter alongside a 'Season of Constraint' field. The firm capacity corresponding to the substations 'Season of Constraint' is used as the firm capacity. For those with no Season of Constraint, the Winter Firm Capacity is used.

  • Electricity North West Heatmap Tool Data - Primary Substation Demand Headrooms. Data in .xlsx format, with the 'Primary Headroom Data' sheet being manually converted into a .csv file. This data lacks firm capacity data for each substation so only uses the 'Firm Demand Headroom (MW)' field.

The 'Demand Headroom Rating' for each substation is either 'RED', 'AMBER' or 'GREEN', the classfications for each determined by the substations DNO. The method of classifying substations is not always clear and is not necessarily consistent between DNOs. Electricity North West did not have such a rating so was added: set to 'RED' if headroom = 0, 'AMBER' if headroom less than 2 MVA and 'GREEN' otherwise.

Supplementary Grid Data

The Northern Power Grid and Electricity North West capacity datasets do not include geospatial data for its substations, so supplementary data is used to match each substations capacity data to its corresponding Latitude/Longitude Coordinates. This data is combined into a single .csv file, to be used directly in the application.

Geo Boundaries Data

Local Authority District Boundaries

Local Authority Districts (LADs) are present throughout the application as a high level way of grouping and visualizing data. Due to the ever evolving nature of these boundaries, with additions and deletions fairly common each year, they are a source of potential error in the application (mismatches and missing boundary data). In an attempt to mitigate this, boundary information from multiple years is often utilized.

  • LAD Boundaries - December 2022
  • LAD Boundaries - December 2020

The boundary type chosen was 'BUC' or ultra generalised boundaries (500m), clipped to the coastline. This data is used in Shapefile (.shp) format and GEOJSON format depending on the requirements. Shapefile format for plotting all boundaries at once and GEOJSON when plotting a single LAD. (The GEOJSON was manually seperated into its constituent parts for efficiency purposes.)

Postcode Boundaries Data

Postcode Boundary data, plotted for each LAD, at postcode district and sector levels was compiled using NSUL data from the Office for National Statistics by Mark, accessed at https://longair.net/blog/2021/08/23/open-data-gb-postcode-unit-boundaries/.



Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) Data

The Energy Performance Certificate is required for any dwelling that is to be sold or rented. It contains information regarding the energy performance of a home: physical features (i.e.. wall insulation levels) and recommended improvements to increase the EPC rating (score 0-100).

When requesting data for an individual dwelling requests to the following endpoints are made:

  • https://epc.opendatacommunitie‘s.org/api/v1/domestic/search
  • https://epc.opendatacommunities.org/api/v1/domestic/recommendations/:lmk-key

This developer API requires an api key registered to an email address. Full documentation for the API can be found at epc.opendatacommunties.org



EPC data when used at local authority district level, was downloaded offline and saved in a reduced feature format for space efficiency. This data is used in all calculations at LAD level and is used to calculate a Heat Pump Readiness (HPR) Rating for every EPC.

This data is downloaded as a .zip, with folders for each district containing a .csv file of all EPCs classified at this district. It should be noted here that these .csv files were found to contain a small amount of EPCs corresponding to homes and postcodes not located within the district (geographically), it is likely these are due to errors and highly insignificant over the whole set.

Limitations of the EPC

It is important to discuss some of the limitations of using the EPC dataset to assess a properties energy performance and subsequent 'heat pump readiness'. Such criticisms lie in their inconsistencies and inaccuracies, which can stem from inconsistent evaluations between different assessors and the overall assumptive nature of the methodologies used to produce the EPC.

  • Bunching: where the manipulation of input data results in the performance of properties otherwise below a rating band stagnate just above the threshold, results in misclassifications of properties energy performance.
  • Research suggests an EPC rating can vary by 4-11 points for similar properties, stemming from inconsistent evaluations. [1][2]
  • Outdated EPCs: We found that, on average, approximately 30% of certificates for a local authority district have expired (10+ years old). This makes these certificates more unreliable as the likelihood of home upgrades to improve energy performance not shown on the old EPC increases.
  • Assumptions regarding the energy efficiency levels of certain property features. I.E.. it was found that default U-values used for solid brick walls underestimate thermal performance by ~1/3.[3]

For a more detailed report on the inconsistencies and inaccuracies of the EPC see Section 4.2 of the tools written thesis

Heat Pump Readiness (HPR) Rating Calculation

A properties HPR rating is calculated using information from the EPC.

The specific features of the EPC considered were: 'window-energy-efficiency', 'wall-energy-efficiency', 'roof-energy-efficiency', 'floor-energy-efficiency' and overall 'current-energy-efficiency' (the headline epc rating). These specific features of a dwelling give an idea to its levels of insulation, which can be a large factor in determining how effective and efficient an installed heat pump can be, hence the derived 'heat pump readiness' metric.

The HPR was calibrated around an value of 1, above indicating an 'ideal candidate'for a heat pump. Drawing from the Boiler Upgrade Schemes requirements and the high insulation standards for efficient heat pump operation, it was concluded that a dwelling with at least ratings of 'Good'for wall and roof/loft efficiency ratings are likely to be good heat pump candidate and should have a HPR rating >1. This was considered in the HPR calculation by having these 2 features weighted double. Each feature rating has 5 possible values: [very-good, good, average, poor, very-poor], corresponding to [0, 1, 3, 6, 10] points in the model, with poorer ratings contributing more heavily to the score. The headline 'current-energy-efficiency'value, is inverted and scaled. A score of 100 translating to 0 points, with every -10 in score corresponding to +0.5 points in the model. All scores were summed to give a final score which could range [0, 70], the lower the value the more heat pump ready. This score was then translated linearly to a rating [1.2, 0.2] using numpys 'interp'function, meaning a final score of <14 corresponded to a hpr value of ≥ 1. Ratings ≥ 1.1 were deemed 'Strong Candidates', ≥ 1 'Good Candidates', ≥ 0.7 'Some Improvements Needed'and <0.7 'Major Improvements Needed'.

Uncertainties in the HPR Calculation

As the Heat Pump Readiness Rating is derived from a properties EPC, it shares some of the issues detailed in EPC Data. The assumption of U-values (insulation measure) for solid walls directy affects the accuracy of the 'walls-energy-efficiency' rating in the EPC, used as a doubly weighted component of the HPR calculation. Other factors that would impact a homes 'heat pump readiness' include property type, age and internal heating apparatus, which are not considered in this HPR calculation. The increased electrical demand from heat pump uptake is not factored into the rating but is considered on the Grid Page. For a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the HPR rating see section 4.3 of thetools written thesis.

Heat Pump Adoption Data

Data regarding heat pump adoption was requested through information.rights@ofgem.gov.uk and was accepted under the Environmental Information Regulations 2004 (EIR). This data was 'Cumulative Number of Heat Pumps Installed by Local Authority by Year in Great Britain' and was seperated by air source or ground source installations. These heat pump installations were all under the RHI (Renewable Heat Incentive) scheme, which was recently replaced by the Boiler Upgrade Scheme (BUS), both goverment ran.
